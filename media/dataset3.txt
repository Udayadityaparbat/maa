Q1:
Q: What is a period and why does it happen?
A: A period (menstruation) is when the inner lining of the uterus sheds through the vagina as blood. It happens every month because the uterus prepares itself for a possible pregnancy. When no pregnancy occurs, the lining breaks down and exits the body. It usually lasts 2–7 days and comes roughly every 21–35 days. It is completely normal and healthy — every girl and woman goes through it.
KEYWORDS: what is period, why period happens, menstruation basics, uterus, monthly cycle
SOURCE: WHO; NIH MedlinePlus
---

Q2:
Q: At what age does a girl get her first period?
A: The first period (called menarche) usually happens between ages 11 and 14. Some girls get it as early as 9 and some as late as 16 — both can be normal. In India, the average age of first period is around 12.5 to 13 years. It usually comes 2–3 years after breast development begins. If a girl has not had her first period by age 15 and has other signs of puberty, she should see a doctor.
KEYWORDS: first period age, menarche, puberty, India, 12 years, 13 years, when period starts
SOURCE: WHO; Indian Journal of Pediatrics 2010
---

Q3:
Q: How many days does a normal period last?
A: A normal period lasts between 2 to 7 days. Most girls bleed heavily in the first 1–2 days and lighter toward the end. Bleeding for less than 2 days or more than 7 days regularly may need a doctor's attention.
KEYWORDS: period duration, how many days, 5 days, 7 days, period length
SOURCE: ACOG; NIH
---

Q4:
Q: Is it normal if my period is irregular?
A: Yes, especially in the first 1–3 years after your first period. The body's hormonal system is still maturing, so cycles can be shorter, longer, or skipped entirely. After 2–3 years, most girls develop a more regular pattern. However, if periods remain very irregular beyond that, or if you miss 3 or more periods in a row (and are not pregnant), you should consult a doctor.
KEYWORDS: irregular period, normal, first years, teenage, cycle variation, skip period
SOURCE: ACOG; AAP
---

Q5:
Q: How do I know when my next period is coming?
A: Track your cycle on a calendar or a free app (like Flo or Clue). Mark the first day of each period. After 3–4 months you can see a pattern — most girls' cycles repeat every 21–35 days. Apps can predict your next period automatically. Also watch for signs: mild cramps, breast tenderness, bloating, or increased discharge a few days before your period is a common signal.
KEYWORDS: predict period, track period, calendar, Flo app, Clue app, cycle tracking, next period
SOURCE: ACOG; Reproductive Health
---

Q6:
Q: Can I get my period twice in one month?
A: Yes, this can happen. If your cycle is shorter than 28 days (say, 21–23 days), you may bleed twice in one calendar month — this is not the same as two separate periods in one cycle. True "double bleeding" (two periods within one cycle) is abnormal and may signal a hormonal issue or a condition like PCOS or thyroid problems. If it happens repeatedly, see a doctor.
KEYWORDS: two periods one month, double period, short cycle, 21 days, hormonal
SOURCE: ACOG; Mayo Clinic
---

Q7:
Q: Is period blood the same as normal blood?
A: Not exactly. Menstrual fluid is a mix of blood, cells from the uterine lining (endometrium), mucus, and vaginal secretions. It is not purely blood — which is why it may look darker (dark red, brownish) than a cut on your finger, and why it does not clot the same way. It is NOT dirty — it is normal body tissue.
KEYWORDS: period blood composition, dark blood, mucus, uterine lining, normal, not dirty
SOURCE: ACOG; Planned Parenthood
---

Q8:
Q: Why is my period blood sometimes brown or dark?
A: Dark brown or blackish period blood is completely normal and very common — especially at the beginning or end of your period. It is simply older blood that took longer to leave the body. As blood sits in the uterus or vagina for a while, it oxidizes (reacts with air) and turns darker. Fresh red blood = newer; dark brown blood = older. Both are normal.
KEYWORDS: brown period blood, dark blood, old blood, oxidized, beginning end period, normal
SOURCE: Planned Parenthood; NHS
---

Q9:
Q: Can stress delay or stop my period?
A: Yes. Stress is one of the most common reasons for a late or missed period. When you are under physical or emotional stress, your body produces a hormone called cortisol, which can disrupt the hormonal signals that control your cycle. Exam stress, family problems, illness, or sudden weight changes can all delay a period by a few days to a few weeks. Once the stress reduces, your period usually returns. If periods are missed for 3+ months, see a doctor.
KEYWORDS: stress delay period, late period stress, cortisol, exam stress, missed period, anxiety
SOURCE: NIH; ACOG
---

Q10:
Q: What is PMS?
A: PMS stands for Premenstrual Syndrome. It refers to a group of physical and emotional symptoms that appear in the 1–2 weeks before a period and go away once the period starts. Common symptoms include: bloating, breast soreness, headaches, acne, tiredness, mood swings, irritability, and food cravings. Up to 75% of menstruating girls experience some PMS. It is caused by hormonal changes and is real — not imagined.
KEYWORDS: PMS, premenstrual syndrome, bloating, mood swings, before period, symptoms, irritable, hormonal
SOURCE: ACOG; NIH
---

Q11:
Q: Is it normal to have clots in period blood?
A: Small blood clots (smaller than a 10-rupee coin) are completely normal, especially on heavy days. Clots form when blood pools and coagulates before leaving the body. Passing large clots (bigger than a 50-rupee coin) regularly, or very frequently throughout your period, may indicate heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia) and should be discussed with a doctor.
KEYWORDS: blood clots period, clotting, normal clots, large clots, heavy bleeding, menorrhagia
SOURCE: ACOG; NHS
---

Q12:
Q: Does everyone know when I have my period?
A: No. Your period is a private matter. If you use a pad, tampon, or menstrual cup correctly and change it regularly, there will be no visible sign or smell that others can detect. Wearing dark clothing on heavy days, carrying extra pads in a small pouch, and using a toilet for changes keeps it completely private. You do not have to tell anyone unless you choose to.
KEYWORDS: private period, does anyone know, smell, visible, secret, privacy, embarrassed
SOURCE: Planned Parenthood; School Health Guidelines
---

Q13:
Q: Can I swim or play sports during my period?
A: Yes, absolutely. Swimming and sports are safe and even helpful during your period. Exercise releases endorphins, which are natural painkillers that reduce cramps. For swimming, use a tampon or menstrual cup (water pressure reduces flow while submerged, so there is usually no leakage). There is no medical reason to stop any physical activity during menstruation.
KEYWORDS: swim period, sports period, exercise, swimming tampon, active period, cramps exercise
SOURCE: ACOG; Planned Parenthood
---

Q14:
Q: What is spotting?
A: Spotting is very light bleeding that happens outside of your regular period — just a few drops or a light stain, usually pink, light red, or brown. It can happen due to ovulation (around mid-cycle), implantation (early pregnancy), hormonal fluctuations, or stress. Occasional spotting is usually harmless. If spotting happens frequently or is accompanied by pain, it should be checked by a doctor.
KEYWORDS: spotting, light bleeding, pink discharge, brown spotting, between periods, ovulation, implantation
SOURCE: ACOG; Mayo Clinic
---

Q15:
Q: When should I see a doctor about my period?
A: See a doctor if:
• Your period has not started by age 15
• You miss 3 or more periods in a row (and are not pregnant)
• Your period suddenly becomes much heavier or lighter than usual
• You have severe pain that stops you from daily activities
• Your period lasts more than 7 days regularly
• You pass large clots frequently
• You have bleeding between periods or after sex
• You have unusual discharge with odor, itching, or burning
KEYWORDS: doctor period, when to see doctor, medical help, severe cramps, heavy period, missed period, warning signs
SOURCE: ACOG; WHO
---

Q16:
Q: Is my first period always heavy?
A: Not necessarily. The first period can be very light — just a few drops or light spotting — or it can be moderately heavy. There is no standard for the first period. It may be irregular in amount, color (ranging from light pink to dark brown), and length. Subsequent periods gradually become more regular over 1–2 years. Whatever your first period looks like, it is most likely normal.
KEYWORDS: first period heavy light, menarche, what to expect, spotting, irregular first period
SOURCE: ACOG; Planned Parenthood
---

Q17:
Q: Does everyone get PMS?
A: Not everyone. Studies show up to 75% of menstruating girls experience at least some PMS symptoms, but the type and severity vary greatly. Some girls barely notice any changes before their period. Others experience significant physical and emotional symptoms. Severe PMS that interferes with daily life is called PMDD (Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder) and affects about 3–8% of girls — it is a real medical condition that can be treated.
KEYWORDS: everyone PMS, PMS percentage, PMDD, mild PMS, severity, mood, hormonal
SOURCE: ACOG; DSM-5
---

Q18:
Q: Can boys understand what a period feels like?
A: Boys cannot physically experience menstruation, but they can and should understand what it is and what girls go through. When boys are educated about menstruation, they are less likely to tease or bully girls and more likely to be supportive. Research shows that mixed-gender menstrual health education reduces stigma for girls significantly. Talking about it openly — in appropriate settings — helps break down unnecessary shame.
KEYWORDS: boys period, boys understanding, stigma, education, supportive, teasing, gender
SOURCE: UNICEF; UNESCO; Dasra Report
---

Q19:
Q: What is vaginal discharge and is it normal?
A: Yes, vaginal discharge is completely normal. It is a clear or white fluid produced by the cervix and vaginal walls. It keeps the vagina healthy and clean. The amount and consistency change throughout the cycle — thicker and stretchy (like egg white) around ovulation; thicker and white just before a period. Normal discharge has no strong smell. Yellow/green, chunky, or foul-smelling discharge with itching may indicate an infection and should be seen by a doctor.
KEYWORDS: discharge normal, white discharge, clear discharge, vaginal fluid, ovulation discharge, infection, egg white
SOURCE: ACOG; NHS
---

Q20:
Q: Does menstruation mean I can now get pregnant?
A: Once menstruation begins, the body is capable of ovulation — releasing an egg. This means pregnancy is biologically possible. However, in the first 1–2 years after menarche, ovulation may be irregular and cycles may be anovulatory (no egg released). Even so, it is possible to become pregnant before the second period. Comprehensive sex education is important so girls understand their fertility and body changes correctly.
KEYWORDS: period pregnancy, fertile after period, menarche fertility, ovulation, conception, sex education
SOURCE: ACOG; WHO; Planned Parenthood

Q21:
Q: How often should I change my pad?
A: Change your pad every 4 to 6 hours, even if it is not fully soaked. On heavy days, change more often. Never leave a pad on for more than 8 hours as this causes bacterial growth, skin rash, bad odor, and risk of infection. Always wash your hands before and after changing. Carrying an extra pad in your school bag is a good habit.
KEYWORDS: pad change, how often, 4 hours, 6 hours, hygiene, bacteria, skin rash, infection
SOURCE: MoHFW India; ACOG
---

Q22:
Q: How do I wash my private area during my period?
A: Wash only the OUTSIDE (vulva) with clean water 2–3 times a day. Use mild unscented soap on the outer skin only. NEVER put soap, water, or any product INSIDE the vagina — it is self-cleaning and disrupting it causes infections. Always wipe from front to back after using the toilet. Dry gently with a clean cloth or tissue.
KEYWORDS: wash vagina period, vulva cleaning, soap inside vagina, front to back, hygiene, clean
SOURCE: ACOG; WHO; MoHFW India
---

Q23:
Q: Can I bathe during my period?
A: Yes — absolutely. Bathing during your period is not only safe but strongly recommended. It removes blood, sweat, and bacteria from your body. Warm water also helps relax the uterus and reduce cramps. There is zero scientific evidence that bathing is harmful during menstruation. This is a common myth.
KEYWORDS: bath during period, shower, bathe, safe, myth, cramps, warm water
SOURCE: ACOG; MoHFW India
---

Q24:
Q: How do I get rid of period smell?
A: The mild metallic smell of menstrual blood is normal. To manage it:
• Change pads every 4–6 hours — the main cause of odor is old blood on a pad.
• Bathe once or twice daily.
• Wash the external vulva with clean water.
• Wear breathable cotton underwear.
• Avoid scented sprays or perfumes inside the vagina — they disrupt natural pH and CAUSE odor long-term.
A strong fishy smell may indicate bacterial vaginosis (BV) — see a doctor.
KEYWORDS: period smell, odor, how to reduce odor, pad smell, vaginal spray, fishy smell, BV, cotton underwear
SOURCE: ACOG; Planned Parenthood
---

Q25:
Q: How do I dispose of a used pad properly?
A: Wrap the used pad in its wrapper or newspaper, fold it, and put it in a covered dustbin. DO NOT flush pads down the toilet — they block pipes and damage sanitation systems. Do not leave them in open places. At school, use the designated covered bin in the toilet. If there is no bin, carry a small disposal bag in your school bag.
KEYWORDS: dispose pad, how to throw pad, flush toilet, dustbin, wrap, school disposal, environment
SOURCE: MoHFW India 2019; CPCB India
---

Q26:
Q: What do I do if I get my period at school and have no pad?
A: Stay calm. Options:
1. Fold toilet paper and place it inside your underwear temporarily.
2. Ask a trusted female friend, female teacher, or school nurse for a pad.
3. Tie a sweater or jacket around your waist if there is a stain.
4. Use the school toilet to clean up.
Always keep a spare pad in your bag, pencil case, or wallet for emergencies. Schools should also have emergency pads available — it is okay to ask.
KEYWORDS: period at school, no pad, emergency, toilet paper, ask teacher, spare pad, stain, school nurse
SOURCE: UNICEF; School Health Guidelines
---

Q27:
Q: How do I use a tampon? Is it safe?
A: A tampon is a cotton tube inserted into the vagina to absorb period blood internally. Steps:
1. Wash your hands.
2. Sit on the toilet or put one foot on it.
3. Relax your body — tensing makes insertion difficult.
4. Follow the angle of your vagina (tilted back) and insert fully.
5. Change every 4–8 hours. NEVER leave in more than 8 hours (risk of TSS).
Tampons are safe when used correctly. They do NOT break your hymen or affect virginity. Start with the smallest size (junior/light) if new to tampons.
KEYWORDS: tampon use, how to insert tampon, safe tampon, TSS, virgin tampon, 8 hours, cotton
SOURCE: FDA; ACOG; Planned Parenthood
---

Q28:
Q: What is a menstrual cup and how do I use it?
A: A menstrual cup is a small, flexible silicone cup inserted into the vagina to COLLECT (not absorb) menstrual blood. How to use:
1. Wash hands and sterilize the cup.
2. Fold the cup (C-fold or punch-down fold) and insert like a tampon.
3. It pops open inside and creates a seal — should feel comfortable.
4. Empty every 8–12 hours, rinse with water, reinsert.
5. At end of period, boil in water for 5 minutes to sterilize.
One cup lasts 5–10 years. Cost: ₹300–800 one time. It is eco-friendly, economic, and very safe.
KEYWORDS: menstrual cup, how to use cup, silicone cup, fold insert, eco-friendly, reusable, boil sterilize, affordable
SOURCE: Lancet Public Health 2019; WHO
---

Q29:
Q: Is it okay to wear a pad at night while sleeping?
A: Yes. Wear a longer, overnight pad designed for sleeping — they are wider at the back to prevent leaks when lying down. Change the pad before going to bed and immediately after waking up. Sleeping with a pad for 6–8 hours overnight is fine. Do NOT sleep with a tampon overnight unless you insert it fresh right before sleeping AND wake within 8 hours.
KEYWORDS: overnight pad, pad sleeping, night period, tampon sleeping, overnight protection, back leakage
SOURCE: ACOG; MoHFW India
---

Q30:
Q: How do I clean period stains from my underwear or clothes?
A: Act quickly — fresh stains are easier to remove:
1. Rinse with COLD water immediately (never hot — heat sets the stain).
2. Rub with soap or apply salt on the wet stain and scrub gently.
3. Hydrogen peroxide (3%) can lift dried stains — apply, let sit 5 minutes, rinse.
4. Soak in cold water for 30 minutes if the stain has dried.
5. Wash normally with laundry detergent afterward.
Period stains on clothes are not shameful — they happen to every girl. Keep a stain remover stick in your bag if you are worried.
KEYWORDS: period stain, blood stain, underwear stain, cold water, remove stain, salt, hydrogen peroxide, laundry
SOURCE: Planned Parenthood; School Health Guidelines
---

Q31:
Q: How do I manage my period while traveling or on a long trip?
A: Planning ahead makes traveling easy:
• Pack extra pads/tampons/cups — more than you think you'll need.
• Carry a small ziplock bag for disposal when bins are unavailable.
• Wear dark-colored clothes on heavy days.
• Stay hydrated and carry pain relief tablets (ibuprofen) if you get cramps.
• Menstrual cups are ideal for travel — no disposal issue, lasts 12 hours.
• If traveling internationally, remember product availability varies by country.
KEYWORDS: period travel, trip period, long journey, manage period, menstrual cup travel, pack pads, cramps travel
SOURCE: Planned Parenthood; Travel Health Guidelines
---

Q32:
Q: Is it safe to use cloth pads? How do I maintain them?
A: Clean cloth can be safely used if:
• Washed with soap and hot water after every use.
• Dried in DIRECT SUNLIGHT — sunlight kills bacteria naturally.
• Stored in a clean, dry place — NOT in a damp bathroom or folded wet.
• Never shared with another person.
Wet, unclean, or improperly stored cloth dramatically increases the risk of vaginal and reproductive tract infections. Studies in rural India show this is a common cause of infections in girls who use cloth without proper hygiene.
KEYWORDS: cloth pad, reusable cloth, safe cloth, wash cloth pad, sunlight dry, infection cloth, rural India
SOURCE: Indian Journal of Community Medicine 2016; UNICEF WASH
---

Q33:
Q: Do I need to use feminine hygiene sprays or douches?
A: NO. Do NOT use vaginal sprays, douches, scented wipes, or perfumed soaps inside or near your vagina during menstruation (or at any time). The vagina is self-cleaning and maintains its own healthy pH (3.8–4.5). These products:
• Disrupt the natural bacterial balance (Lactobacillus)
• Raise the vaginal pH, making infections MORE likely
• Can cause bacterial vaginosis, yeast infections, and irritation
• May mask symptoms of actual infections
Plain water on the external area is all you need.
KEYWORDS: douche, feminine spray, vaginal wash, pH balance, Lactobacillus, infection risk, scented products, not safe
SOURCE: ACOG; CDC; WHO
---

Q34:
Q: What underwear is best to wear during a period?
A: During your period:
• Wear cotton underwear — breathable, reduces moisture, prevents bacterial growth.
• Choose a comfortable, full-coverage style (not thongs) to hold the pad securely.
• Dark-colored underwear can hide accidental stains.
• Have a dedicated set of older underwear you are not worried about staining.
• Avoid tight synthetic fabrics that trap heat and moisture — this worsens infection risk and skin irritation.
Period underwear (absorbent underwear that acts like a pad) is also available in India now and works well as backup protection.
KEYWORDS: underwear period, cotton underwear, dark underwear, period underwear, thong, synthetic, breathable, skin irritation
SOURCE: ACOG; Planned Parenthood
---

Q35:
Q: How do I stop leaking at night?
A: To prevent nighttime leaks:
• Use extra-long overnight pads designed for lying down.
• Position the pad further toward the back of your underwear.
• Wear fitted briefs that hold the pad securely.
• Sleep on your side or back (sleeping on the stomach can push the pad forward).
• Place a dark towel under your sheet on heavy nights as extra protection.
• If leaks still happen, a menstrual cup or period underwear worn with a pad provides better security.
KEYWORDS: period leaking night, overnight pad, nighttime leak, sleep period, prevent leak, back leakage, menstrual cup
SOURCE: School Health Guidelines; Planned Parenthood

Q36:
Q: Why do I get cramps during my period?
A: Period cramps happen because of chemicals called prostaglandins, which are released by the uterus as it sheds its lining. Prostaglandins cause the uterine muscles to contract (squeeze) to push the lining out. These contractions temporarily cut off blood flow, causing pain. The cramps are usually felt in the lower abdomen, lower back, and sometimes thighs. Higher prostaglandin levels = stronger cramps. It is real pain — not imagined.
KEYWORDS: period cramps, prostaglandins, uterine contractions, lower abdomen pain, why cramps, real pain, dysmenorrhea
SOURCE: ACOG; Obstetrics & Gynecology 2006
---

Q37:
Q: What helps with period cramps?
A: Evidence-based remedies that actually work:
• Ibuprofen or naproxen (NSAIDs) — most effective; take at the FIRST sign of cramps, not after pain is severe.
• Warm/hot water bottle on the lower abdomen — relaxes uterine muscles.
• Light exercise (walking, yoga) — releases endorphins, natural painkillers.
• Warm herbal teas (ginger, chamomile) — have mild anti-inflammatory properties.
• Staying hydrated — reduces bloating which worsens cramp pain.
• Omega-3 rich foods (fish, flaxseed, walnuts) — reduce prostaglandin production.
If cramps are so severe you cannot attend school or do daily activities, see a doctor — this is not normal and is treatable.
KEYWORDS: cramp relief, ibuprofen, hot water bottle, yoga, ginger tea, exercise, omega-3, home remedies, pain relief
SOURCE: Cochrane Review 2015; ACOG; J Educ Health Promot 2018

Q38:
Q: What is PCOS and can a teenage girl have it?
A: Yes, PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) can and does affect teenage girls. It is a hormonal disorder where the ovaries produce too many male hormones (androgens), disrupting normal egg development and ovulation.
Signs in teens:
• Irregular or very infrequent periods (fewer than 8 per year)
• Acne that is hard to control
• Excess hair on face, chin, or body (hirsutism)
• Weight gain, especially around the belly
• Scalp hair thinning
PCOS cannot be cured but is very manageable with lifestyle changes (diet, exercise), hormonal medications, and medical care. Diagnosis is usually made by blood tests and ultrasound.
KEYWORDS: PCOS teenage, polycystic ovary, hormonal disorder, acne, irregular period, facial hair, weight gain, insulin, diagnosis
SOURCE: ACOG; ASRM 2023; Indian Journal of Endocrinology
---

Q39:
Q: Is heavy bleeding during periods normal?
A: Some heaviness on the first 1–2 days is normal. But you should see a doctor if:
• You soak through a pad or tampon every hour for 2 or more hours in a row
• Your period lasts more than 7 days
• You pass clots larger than a 50-rupee coin
• You feel very tired, dizzy, or pale (signs of anemia from blood loss)
Heavy bleeding (menorrhagia) has treatable causes — fibroids, hormonal imbalance, thyroid problems — and you should not just "manage" it silently.
KEYWORDS: heavy period, menorrhagia, soaking pads, large clots, anemia, dizziness, fatigue, see doctor, normal heavy
SOURCE: ACOG; BMJ 2007
---

Q40:
Q: Why do I feel nauseous or get diarrhea during my period?
A: The same prostaglandins that cause uterine cramps can enter the bloodstream and affect the digestive system — causing nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, and stomach pain. This is especially common on the first day. It is real and caused by biology, not anxiety.
Help:
• Take ibuprofen early — it reduces prostaglandins throughout the body.
• Eat small, light meals on crampy days.
• Avoid greasy or spicy food when nauseous.
• Warm ginger tea soothes nausea.
• Stay hydrated to replace fluids lost through diarrhea.
KEYWORDS: nausea period, diarrhea period, vomiting, prostaglandins, digestive system, stomach pain, ginger, ibuprofen
SOURCE: ACOG; NIH; Journal of Midwifery & Women's Health
---

Q41:
Q: Can my period make me anemic?
A: Yes. Blood loss during menstruation removes iron from the body. If the iron is not replaced through food or supplements, iron-deficiency anemia develops. Signs of anemia: extreme tiredness, weakness, dizziness, pale skin, shortness of breath, difficulty concentrating. In India, 57% of adolescent girls are anemic (NFHS-5). Girls with heavy periods are at especially high risk. Eating iron-rich foods daily and getting blood tests annually is important.
KEYWORDS: anemia period, iron deficiency, blood loss, tired, dizzy, pale, NFHS-5, India anemia, iron food
SOURCE: NFHS-5 2021; WHO; Indian Pediatrics
---

Q42:
Q: Can periods affect my skin and cause acne?
A: Yes. Hormonal changes around your period directly affect skin:
• Before period (luteal phase): progesterone causes increased oil (sebum) production → clogged pores → pimples.
• Estrogen drops just before your period, reducing skin plumpness and barrier function.
• Androgens (higher in PCOS) cause persistent, severe acne on jaw, chin, and neck.
Tips: cleanse face twice daily, avoid touching your face, don't pop pimples. If acne is severe and cycles with your period, see a doctor — it may indicate PCOS.
KEYWORDS: period acne, pimples period, hormonal acne, progesterone skin, oily skin, PCOS acne, jaw chin acne
SOURCE: ACOG; J Am Acad Dermatology; ASRM
---

Q43:
Q: Why do I get headaches or migraines during my period?
A: Menstrual migraines are common and are caused by the drop in estrogen that occurs just before your period begins. Estrogen affects serotonin levels and pain pathways in the brain. When estrogen falls sharply, some girls experience migraines — often without an aura (visual disturbance), very painful, and lasting 1–3 days.
Management:
• Ibuprofen or naproxen taken at the start of headache.
• Stay hydrated (dehydration worsens headaches).
• Rest in a dark, quiet room.
• Identify and avoid personal triggers (certain foods, bright lights).
If migraines are severe and recurrent, a neurologist or gynecologist can prescribe preventive treatment.
KEYWORDS: period headache, menstrual migraine, estrogen drop, serotonin, migraine no aura, dehydration, pain relief
SOURCE: Cephalalgia Journal; ACOG; Neurology
---

Q44:
Q: What is endometriosis? Can I have it?
A: Endometriosis is a condition where tissue like the uterine lining grows outside the uterus — on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, or other organs. It causes:
• Very severe period pain that gets worse over time (not better with normal painkillers)
• Pain during or after sex
• Heavy periods
• Chronic pelvic pain even between periods
• Difficulty conceiving (in some women)
It affects 1 in 10 women of reproductive age. It can begin in teenage years. It is frequently dismissed as "bad period pain" — which delays diagnosis by 7–10 years. If your pain is severe and does not respond to ibuprofen, ask your doctor to investigate.
KEYWORDS: endometriosis, what is it, teenage endometriosis, severe pain, chronic pelvic pain, uterine tissue outside, misdiagnosis, 7 years
SOURCE: WHO 2023; Lancet; ACOG
---

Q45:
Q: Is it normal to feel extremely tired during my period?
A: Yes. Period fatigue is real and has multiple causes:
1. Blood and iron loss — even mild anemia causes exhaustion.
2. Prostaglandins — the same chemicals causing cramps also cause body-wide fatigue.
3. Disrupted sleep — from pain or discomfort.
4. Hormonal shifts — progesterone has a sedating effect; its drop before periods causes sleep disturbance.
Tips: rest more on heavy days, eat iron-rich foods, stay hydrated, avoid excessive caffeine (worsens fatigue rebound).
Extreme tiredness (unable to get out of bed, pale, dizzy) may indicate anemia — get a blood test.
KEYWORDS: tired during period, fatigue, exhaustion, iron loss, anemia, prostaglandins, sleep disruption, progesterone
SOURCE: ACOG; NIH; J Women's Health
---

Q46:
Q: Does the thyroid affect my period?
A: Yes. The thyroid gland controls the body's metabolism, and thyroid problems are a very common cause of period irregularity.
• Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid): causes heavy periods, very long cycles, or missed periods. Also causes tiredness, weight gain, feeling cold, and hair loss.
• Hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid): causes very light, infrequent periods or missed periods. Also causes weight loss, anxiety, heart palpitations, feeling hot.
If your periods are irregular AND you notice any of the above symptoms, ask your doctor for a TSH blood test. Thyroid problems are common in Indian women and are easily treated.
KEYWORDS: thyroid period, hypothyroidism periods, hyperthyroidism, TSH test, irregular period thyroid, India, weight gain
SOURCE: Krassas GE, Eur J Endocrinol 2010; ACOG
---

Q47:
Q: Can I take painkillers for period pain?
A: Yes. Ibuprofen (400–600 mg) and naproxen are the most effective medicines for period pain. They work by blocking prostaglandin production — the actual cause of cramps. Best results when taken:
• At the first sign of cramps (even before pain is severe)
• With food to protect the stomach
• Every 6–8 hours for the first 1–2 days of your period
Do NOT take on an empty stomach. Paracetamol (Crocin) is less effective for period cramps because it does not block prostaglandins. If over-the-counter medicines don't help, see a doctor — stronger options are available.

Q: Why do I feel so emotional, sad, or angry before my period?
A: This is completely real and has a biological explanation. In the 1–2 weeks before your period (luteal phase), estrogen gradually drops and progesterone rises and then falls. These shifts affect the brain:
• Falling estrogen reduces serotonin — the neurotransmitter responsible for mood stability and happiness.
• Progesterone fluctuations affect GABA receptors in the brain — causing anxiety and irritability.
• Mood can shift quickly — crying easily, snapping at people, feeling hopeless — and then improving as soon as the period starts.
These are real hormonal effects on brain chemistry. They are not weakness, over-sensitivity, or "drama." Tracking your cycle helps you recognize the pattern and respond with more self-compassion.

KEYWORDS: very painful period, worse than friends, severe cramps, endometriosis, adenomyosis, dismissed pain, see gynecologist, not normal
SOURCE: WHO 2023; Lancet; ACOG

Q84:
Q: Is it normal to feel aroused or have increased sexual feelings during some parts of my cycle?
A: Yes — completely normal and biological. Sexual desire (libido) naturally fluctuates with the menstrual cycle:
• HIGHEST around ovulation (mid-cycle): testosterone and estrogen peak, increasing libido. This is evolutionarily designed to coincide with peak fertility.
• LOWER in the luteal phase (after ovulation, before period): progesterone dampens libido in many women.
• VARIABLE during menstruation: some feel arousal from increased blood flow to the pelvic area; others feel reduced desire from pain and discomfort.
These are normal hormonal patterns. Understanding them is part of body literacy — knowing how your own cycle works.
KEYWORDS: libido period, aroused cycle, sexual feelings, ovulation desire, testosterone, progesterone, body literacy, normal cycle, hormones
SOURCE: Endocrine Society; J Sex Research; Planned Parenthood
---

Q85:
Q: I have been told not to wash my hair during my period. Is this true?
A: This is a myth with no scientific basis. Washing your hair during menstruation is completely safe and has no effect on your period, flow, hormones, or health. The origin of this belief is unclear but is common across South Asia and parts of East Asia. In fact, regular hair washing during menstruation is part of good general hygiene. Cold water on the scalp also does not cause harm. You can wash your hair as you normally would throughout your cycle.
KEYWORDS: wash hair period, hair washing myth, South Asia, cold water, safe, hygiene, no effect, cultural belief
SOURCE: ACOG; MoHFW India; NHS

Q86:
Q: Can I go to religious places or pray during my period?
A: Medically and scientifically, there is absolutely no restriction. Menstruation has no effect on a person's spiritual status — that is a matter of biology, not spirituality. Many religious traditions globally do place cultural restrictions on menstruating women, but these are human-made traditions that vary widely even within religions and regions. Many religious scholars across traditions actively argue that such restrictions are misinterpretations. Your health, dignity, and participation in spiritual life are not diminished by your biology.
KEYWORDS: temple during period, prayer, religion menstruation, spiritual purity, cultural restriction, scientific, human right, dignity, mosque church
SOURCE: WHO; UNICEF; Comparative Religious Studies; Indian Constitution

Teachers are generally understanding when you are honest about medical symptoms. If a teacher dismisses your pain, you can involve a school counselor or parent. You have a right to medical comfort during school hours.

Q89:
Q: I am 16 and have never had my period. Should I worry?
A: If you are 16 and have had NO period AND you are also not showing signs of puberty (breast development, pubic hair, growth spurt), you should see a doctor — this is called primary amenorrhea. If you HAVE begun puberty but have no period by age 15, a doctor should be consulted as well. Possible causes range from simple hormonal timing to conditions like Turner syndrome, anatomical issues, or hormonal imbalances — most of which are diagnosable and manageable. A blood test and physical examination by a gynecologist will clarify the situation.
KEYWORDS: no period 16, primary amenorrhea, never had period, puberty signs, doctor, Turner syndrome, hormonal, age 15

Q: Is it normal to feel hungrier before my period?
A: Yes. Food cravings before a period are driven by serotonin dips (prompting carb cravings) and increased metabolic rate in the luteal phase (body burns slightly more calories). Choosing complex carbs over sweets helps stabilize mood.

Q98:
Q: Will my period sync with my friends or roommates?
A: The theory of menstrual synchrony (McClintock effect) has NOT been confirmed by modern research. While widely believed, large-scale studies have found no statistically significant synchronization of cycles among women living together. Any apparent syncing is likely coincidence given cycle variation.
KEYWORDS: period sync, menstrual synchrony, McClintock, friends same period, roommate, myth

Q99:
Q: My period blood smells. Is this an infection?
A: A mild, slightly metallic odor from menstrual blood is normal (due to iron). If the odor is strong, fishy, or foul — especially with yellow/green discharge, itching, or burning — it may indicate bacterial vaginosis or another infection. Change products regularly, wash externally with water, and see a doctor if the strong odor persists.
KEYWORDS: period blood smell, fishy odor, infection, BV, normal smell, metallic, discharge
SOURCE: ACOG; CDC
